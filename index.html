About

Nicolas Morant embarked on his technology journey at age 12, self-teaching HTML and laying the groundwork for a career that would traverse various industries and continents. After earning a BBA and an MBA, his professional journey, initially rooted in marketing, evolved through a series of strategic shifts into operations management, demonstrating his adaptability and keen sense for business dynamics. This evolution was marked by significant leadership roles at companies like Dainese and Corporate Stays, where Nicolas implemented groundbreaking strategies to foster growth and operational efficiency.

Seeking to deepen his expertise, Nicolas ventured back into academia, securing a Master's in Data Science from Harvard University. It was here that his interest in computer vision, machine learning, and AI blossomed, deeply influencing his subsequent work in environmental conservation with WildTrack. His passion for these technologies manifested in his thesis and recent publications, focusing on the application of AI and computer vision in the non-invasive tracking of endangered species. This academic pursuit seamlessly transitioned into impactful conservation work, where Nicolas has been pivotal in developing innovative AI solutions for wildlife protection.

At WildTrack, Nicolas's work is a testament to the power of merging technology with conservation efforts. Leading projects that develop and implement non-invasive tracking technologies, he has become a key figure in the field, showcasing how AI and machine learning can be forces for good in the environmental sector. His involvement goes beyond leadership; as an engineer deeply engaged in coding, Nicolas actively contributes to software engineering, UI/UX design, and the machine learning aspects of projects. Furthermore, his proficiency in CI/CD and DevOps practices enhances project workflows and system reliability, embodying the integration of cutting-edge development practices with the architectural design of robust systems. This comprehensive role underscores his unique position at the intersection of technology, conservation, and machine learning, highlighting his significant impact on the field.
